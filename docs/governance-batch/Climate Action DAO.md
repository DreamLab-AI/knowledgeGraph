## Academic Context

- Brief contextual overview
  - Climate Action DAOs are blockchain-based organisations designed to coordinate collective action on environmental sustainability, leveraging decentralised [[Governance]] and transparent resource allocation
  - These entities emerged from the convergence of climate activism, digital asset management, and distributed ledger technology (DLT), enabling communities to propose, fund, and track climate initiatives without centralised oversight

- Key developments and current state
  - Climate Action DAOs have evolved beyond experimental models to become operational frameworks for climate finance, carbon credit management, and community-driven environmental projects
  - The integration of [[Smart Contract]]s and tokenised incentives has enabled more efficient, auditable, and participatory climate governance

- Academic foundations
  - Theoretical underpinnings draw from commons-based peer production, collective action theory, and blockchain governance literature
  - Scholars have explored the potential of DAOs to address collective action problems in environmental management, particularly in contexts where trust and transparency are paramount

## Current Landscape (2025)

- Industry adoption and implementations
  - Climate Action DAOs are increasingly adopted by environmental NGOs, impact investors, and local authorities seeking to decentralise climate decision-making
  - Notable platforms include KlimaDAO, Toucan Protocol, and the Climate Collective, which facilitate carbon offsetting, climate finance, and community engagement

- UK and North England examples where relevant
  - In Manchester, the Greater Manchester Climate Action DAO has piloted community-led carbon reduction projects, leveraging local stakeholder input and blockchain-based transparency
  - Leeds-based initiatives have explored DAOs for urban sustainability, including energy efficiency schemes and green infrastructure funding
  - Newcastle and Sheffield have seen academic and civic collaborations exploring DAOs for regional climate resilience, with pilot projects in sustainable transport and circular economy models

- Technical capabilities and limitations
  - Climate Action DAOs offer real-time tracking of climate initiatives, automated disbursement of funds, and transparent [[Voting]] mechanisms
  - Limitations include scalability challenges, regulatory uncertainty, and the need for robust digital literacy among participants
  - Interoperability with existing environmental data systems remains a technical hurdle

- Standards and frameworks
  - Industry-standard metrics for climate impact, such as those proposed by the Standard Development Bureau’s PAS (Publicly Available Specification) for DLT-based sustainability, are being adopted to ensure consistency and regulatory [[Compliance]]
  - Frameworks for climate-resilient DAO governance are emerging, embedding climate action into organisational bylaws and decision-making processes
