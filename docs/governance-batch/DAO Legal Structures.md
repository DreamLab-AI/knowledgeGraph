## Academic Context

- Brief contextual overview
	- Decentralised Autonomous Organisations (DAOs) represent a novel organisational form enabled by blockchain technology, characterised by decentralised [[Governance]], [[Smart Contract]] execution, and collective decision-making
	- The academic discourse has evolved from viewing DAOs as purely technical or experimental entities to recognising their need for legal integration and regulatory clarity

- Key developments and current state
	- Recent scholarship has focused on reconciling DAOs with existing legal frameworks, particularly in jurisdictions with mature corporate law such as the United Kingdom
	- The Law Commission of England and Wales has published a scoping paper clarifying how DAOs are treated under English law, concluding that no bespoke legal entity is currently required, but existing structures may be adapted as necessary

- Academic foundations
	- Foundational work includes comparative studies of DAO [[Regulation]] in jurisdictions such as Wyoming, Vermont, and Malta, which have introduced specific legal forms for DAOs
	- The concept of the "legal wrapper" has emerged as a pragmatic solution, allowing DAOs to operate within established legal boundaries while preserving core decentralised principles

## Current Landscape (2025)

- Industry adoption and implementations
	- DAOs are increasingly adopting formal legal structures to manage risk, facilitate [[Compliance]], and interact with traditional financial and legal systems
	- Common legal wrappers include limited liability partnerships, trusts, and foundations, with jurisdictions such as Wyoming (DAO LLCs), Guernsey, and the Cayman Islands offering tailored options

- Notable organisations and platforms
	- Uniswap DAO, ENS DAO, and other prominent Web3 projects have implemented legal wrappers to manage governance and [[Treasury]] operations
	- UK-based DAOs are beginning to adopt similar structures, often referencing English law in their constitutions and participation agreements

- UK and North England examples where relevant
	- Manchester and Leeds have seen a rise in blockchain startups and DAO initiatives, with local legal firms and innovation hubs providing guidance on structuring DAOs under English law
	- Newcastle and Sheffield are emerging as regional centres for blockchain research and development, with academic institutions collaborating on DAO governance and compliance projects

- Technical capabilities and limitations
	- Smart contracts enable automated governance and transparent decision-making, but legal wrappers are necessary to address issues such as liability, asset management, and regulatory compliance
	- The integration of legal wrappers can introduce complexity and potential points of centralisation, requiring careful design to maintain decentralised principles

- Standards and frameworks
	- The Harmony Framework provides a methodical approach to the legal structuring of decentralised organisations, offering adaptable solutions for different jurisdictions
	- Industry standards are evolving, with ongoing efforts to harmonise legal and technical approaches to DAO governance
